## Premise

Our world is real. Modern life continues. People go to work, raise children, get sick, doomscroll, fix trucks, attend church, clock in at warehouses, drink bad coffee, and argue about ordinary things.

Beneath that ordinary surface are layers of beings, forces, hungers, and attentions. They are not neatly supernatural in the human sense. They are not one religion, one mythology, one pantheon, or one war between good and evil. They are ecosystems of scale.

Most people never see enough to understand. They glimpse a shape and give it a familiar name: God, demon, ghost, madness, trauma, bad luck, cancer, hurricane, family curse.

Sometimes they are close enough.

## The Layered World

### 1. The Vast Below

Things so immense that humanity is less than dust to them. Not enemies. Not creators. Not judges. They are not indifferent in a moral sense; they are simply too vast for human importance to register.

Humans are not bugs to them. Bugs are too visible. Humans are closer to germs that do not affect the creature's life.

When one of these things shifts, dreams sour. Fault lines wake. Storms form. Languages rot. A town forgets the name of its dead. No malice is required.

### 2. The Watching Depths

A nearer layer. These beings can notice humans the way humans notice insects: occasionally, vaguely, with curiosity or irritation.

They are not necessarily evil. Some study. Some toy. Some ignore. Some protect for reasons that do not translate cleanly. Some are like children with magnifying glasses. Some are like bored scientists. Some are like gardeners. Some are like landlords.

Drawing their attention is dangerous even when they mean no harm. Their focus has pressure. Their curiosity can flatten a life.

### 3. The Hungry Near-Below

Things closer to the surface: demons, horrors, parasites, old hungers, flesh-wearers, grief-eaters, fever-mouths.

They crave what humans have:

- flesh
- pain
- memory
- worship
- fear
- names
- warmth
- family bonds
- music
- blood
- shame
- grief
- desire

They range from cunning and secret to brutal and stupid. Some build institutions. Some nest in families. Some wait in abandoned places. Some are summoned by accident. Some are inherited.

### 4. The Human-Adjacent

Ghosts, monsters, saints, revenants, land-spirits, old dead things in human shape, things that were once human, things that almost are.

Some are malicious. Some are protective. Some are confused. Some are bound by rules. Some just want to be left alone.

This is the layer most people encounter and misinterpret. A haunting. A monster in the woods. A miracle. A curse. A family story nobody says out loud after dark.

### 5. Humanity

Human beings are neither helpless nor central.

They can resist. They can bargain. They can hide. They can learn hedge magic, make protections, call the attention of kinder powers, close doors, salt thresholds, sing the right songs, bury the right bones, and refuse the terms of things that want them.

But resistance is not control. Most human magic is interface knowledge: people know what to do, not why the universe answers. A waitress can be a saint without knowing it. A dispatcher can use the right phrase without understanding the force it redirects. A mechanic can ward a truck because his grandfather showed him how, not because he understands the whole metaphysics of iron, oil, road-death, and prayer.

There may be experts. They know more, not all. Nobody holds the full machine in their head.

But nobody becomes a god for free. Power always has gravity.

## Public Awareness

This is still our world, but a world under pressure long enough that people have developed habits around the pressure.

Most people do not understand what is underneath. Many would deny believing in it if asked directly. But awareness does not have to look like knowledge. It can look like behavior.

People know weird things happen in the same way they know storms happen, sickness happens, corruption happens, bad luck happens, and old family trouble happens. Some places have rules. Some families have stories. Some teachers, dispatchers, janitors, nurses, board members, preachers, mechanics, and county clerks have seen enough to react differently when the room gets cold.

They believe in:

- God
- ghosts
- curses
- demons
- bad land
- madness
- family secrets
- old wives' tales
- miracles
- weather
- coincidence
- local rules nobody says too loudly
- things everybody knows without admitting they know

They do not grasp the structure beneath it. They do not need to. Most explanations are wrong, but wrong explanations can still help people survive.

The key is partial cultural adaptation, not full revelation. The weird is not public science. It is not open consensus reality. But if impossible things are everywhere, they leave grooves in human behavior: painted-shut windows, porchlights in daylight, words not taught in school, salt nobody mentions, budgets with strange line items, elders who win arguments by getting quiet.

Communities may trust people, offices, and habits they do not understand because not trusting them once went badly. A board may vote against a curriculum update without knowing why, because the old man who has somehow always been there says no and the county has learned to survive by listening. This is not knowledge. It is scar tissue.

## Common Cover Stories

Large disturbances are explained away as:

- hurricanes
- tornadoes
- floods
- earthquakes
- sinkholes
- gas leaks
- electrical fires
- drug episodes
- mass hysteria
- cult violence
- industrial accidents
- illness
- madness
- family annihilation
- animal attacks
- bad luck

Some are honest mistakes. Some are deliberate cover-ups. Some are planted by institutions already compromised.

## Geography and Thin Places

Some areas are closer to the underneath.

Likely thin places:

- deep Appalachian hollers
- abandoned mining towns
- drowned valleys
- old-growth woods
- kudzu-choked ruins
- swamps and blackwater rivers
- towns built over burial grounds, cave systems, mines, or sealed wells
- highway rest stops that should not be there
- corporate campuses on cheap cursed land
- churches that predate their own denominations
- hospitals with too many sealed rooms
- prisons, slaughterhouses, and factories soaked in suffering

Some wild places protect their people. The trees and green world may care in a way that is not human, but still real.

Other places are open wounds.

## Institutions

Institutions can become masks for inhuman forces.

Possible examples:

- government agencies run by demons or demon-adjacent administrators
- corporations directed by executives in contact with deep beings
- churches worshiping something they insist is God
- hospitals harvesting something more than organs
- law enforcement departments that feed people to old bargains
- insurance companies pricing supernatural risk without admitting it exists
- mining, logging, pharmaceutical, and prison corporations as natural occult engines

## Resistance

Resistance is local, practical, and often underfunded.

- families with inherited protections
- hedge witches and rootworkers
- preachers who know the old names but do not say them in church
- mechanics who can ward a truck better than a priest can bless a house
- librarians, nurses, dispatchers, and funeral directors who know patterns
- former cultists
- revenants with unfinished mercy
- monster-hunters who are not glamorous and do not live long
- communities protected by the land itself

They do not have global control. They save who they can.

## Core Tension

Human life is small, fragile, and often unseen — but not meaningless.

The horror is scale. The hope is care.
