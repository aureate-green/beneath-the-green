The coffee in Mara's mug had gone cold, but she drank it anyway. Bitterness was familiar. The kitchen window showed the porch light still burning, though the sun had been up for two hours. She'd forgotten to kill it again. Or maybe she hadn't. Sometimes it felt like the bulb hummed louder when she tried to switch it off, like something on the other side of the glass was holding the filament alive.

Her mother's house needed clearing. Not cleaning—_clearing_. The difference mattered in ways Mara couldn't explain to the realtor who'd called three times this week. The realtor saw square footage, updated plumbing, a fixer-upper with "character." Mara saw the way the hallway mirror had started showing the door behind her a half-second before she turned. She saw the closet where her father's work shirts still hung, though he'd been gone twelve years, and how the sleeves sometimes brushed against her arm when she passed, dry as autumn leaves.

The box in her lap was labeled _KITCHEN – MISC_ in her mother's careful script. Inside: a chipped measuring cup, a bundle of recipe cards tied with twine, a rusted can opener, and a small cloth pouch that smelled of sage and something metallic, like old pennies. Mara's thumb found the drawstring. She didn't open it. She didn't need to. She remembered her mother's hands, rough from garden soil and dishwater, tying that same knot every autumn, placing the pouch on the windowsill above the sink. _Just until the first frost_, she'd say. _Just to keep things settled_.

Outside, a truck backfired down the road. Mara's shoulders tightened. Not a backfire. Something else. A sound like a branch snapping under weight that shouldn't be there. She set the mug down. The liquid inside hadn't rippled.

Her phone buzzed. The realtor again. She let it go to voicemail. Instead, she pulled the pouch onto her lap and untied the knot. Inside: a twist of dried lavender, three smooth river stones, and a lock of hair tied with red thread. Not her mother's. Darker. Coarser. Mara's breath caught. She knew that hair. She'd seen it last on her little brother, Eli, the summer before he wandered off toward the creek and didn't come back the way he should have. They'd found him two days later, shivering on the far bank, talking about "the man who sang with the water." He'd never been right after. Not sick, not broken. Just… elsewhere. He died last winter, quiet in his sleep, at twenty-three. The coroner said natural causes. Mara had held his hand and felt it grow light, like something inside him had finished packing and left through the door.

She re-tied the pouch. Her hands were steady. That was the thing about grief, after a while: it became a kind of muscle memory. You learned to carry it without dropping the other things you still had to hold.

The back door creaked. Not the wind. Mara knew the house's sighs. This was a deliberate sound, the hinge complaining under a slow, careful push. She didn't turn. She kept her eyes on the box, on the recipe card for peach preserves that had her mother's note in the margin: _Add pinch of salt. Keeps the sweet from cloying._

Footsteps. Slow. Wet. Not quite matching the rhythm of human gait. Mara reached into the box, not for a weapon—what would work?—but for the can opener. Its handle was worn smooth by her mother's palm. Familiar weight. Human use. She closed her fingers around it.

The footsteps stopped behind her chair. She could feel the air change. Cooler. Denser. Like walking into a root cellar in July. A smell of wet stone and old moss filled the kitchen. Not unpleasant. Just… present. Insistent.

"Mara." The voice was Eli's. But underneath, like a second track on a worn tape, was the sound of water moving over gravel, of something large shifting in deep, dark places.

She didn't turn. "You're not him."

A pause. The fluorescent light above the sink buzzed, flickered once. "No," the voice agreed. The sound was closer now. She could feel breath on the back of her neck, but it didn't carry warmth. "But he remembers me. And I remember the taste of his worry. The shape of his longing. You carry the same shape."

Mara's thumb found the lever on the can opener. A simple mechanism. Push down, turn. She'd used it a thousand times. "What do you want?"

"The pouch," it said. "It was given. It was kept. It is time."

Her mother's voice echoed in her head, not words, but a feeling: _Some debts aren't paid in money, baby. Some are paid in attention. In remembering the right thing at the right time._

Mara stood. Slowly. She didn't face the thing behind her. She walked to the sink, set the pouch on the windowsill, exactly where her mother always placed it. She picked up the cold coffee mug, poured the dregs down the drain. The water gurgled, too loud in the quiet.

"It's not time," she said, her voice low, rough. "The frost hasn't come."

Silence. The pressure in the room shifted. Not less. But… considering.

"The light," the voice murmured, almost curious. "You leave it burning."

"Porch light's for guidance," Mara said, echoing her mother. "For those who are coming home."

"And for those who are already here?"

She finally turned.

It wore Eli's face, but stretched, softened at the edges like a stone worn by a river. Its eyes were the color of the creek after rain: muddy, deep, holding things that moved just below the surface. It didn't smile. It didn't need to. Its presence was a question, and a weight.

Mara met its gaze. She didn't offer the pouch. She didn't beg. She did the only thing left that felt like hers: she reached past it, to the counter, and picked up the salt jar. Her mother's jar, chipped ceramic, filled with coarse sea salt. She didn't throw it. Didn't chant. She just walked to the back door, the one that had creaked open, and poured a thin, deliberate line of salt across the threshold. A chore. A habit. A boundary drawn in a substance that had kept pests out of the pantry for generations.

The thing that wore her brother's face watched her. It did not cross the line.

"It's not a wall," it said, not unkindly.

"I know," Mara replied. She set the salt jar down. Her hands were still steady. "But it's a reminder. For you. For me. That some doors still have rules."

Outside, the wind picked up. Kudzu slapped against the side of the house. The porch light flickered, then burned steady.

The thing tilted its head. "You will clear the house."

"I will," Mara said.

"And you will leave the pouch."

Mara looked at the windowsill. At the small cloth bundle against the glass. She thought of her mother's hands. Of Eli's laugh, before the creek. Of the realtor's cheerful voice on voicemail, talking about "potential."

"I'll leave it," she said. "Until the frost."

A long moment. The air in the kitchen slowly warmed. The smell of wet stone faded, replaced by the familiar scent of old wood, lemon polish, and the faint, lingering bitterness of cold coffee.

The thing that wore her brother's face stepped back. Not through the door. It simply… thinned. Like mist burning off in morning sun. One breath it was there. The next, the doorway held only the bright, ordinary afternoon, and the sound of a distant lawnmower.

Mara closed the door. She didn't lock it. Some locks were for show. She walked back to the table, to the box labeled _KITCHEN – MISC_. She picked up the recipe card for peach preserves. Read her mother's note again. _Add pinch of salt. Keeps the sweet from cloying._

She tucked the card into her pocket. Then she picked up the cold mug, rinsed it, set it in the drain. The ordinary work of continuing.

Outside, the porch light burned on in the daylight. Mara didn't turn it off. She went back to the box. There were more labels to read. More things to sort. More thresholds, invisible and otherwise, to mind.

The pressure underneath hadn't lifted. It never did. But for now, in this kitchen, with salt on the threshold and a recipe in her pocket and the memory of her mother's hands guiding her own, Mara kept working. The coffee could wait. The house could wait. The things that watched from the layers below could wait, too.

For now.

She picked up the next item. A spoon. Ordinary. Heavy. Human. She held it tight, and began.
