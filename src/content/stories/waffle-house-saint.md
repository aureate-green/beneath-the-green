Rain came down hard enough to make the interstate disappear.

Not all at once. Weather was kinder than that, most nights. It took its time. It blurred the green signs first, then the white lines, then the red taillights of the eighteen-wheelers heading east toward Raleigh or west toward whatever waited past the mountains. By two in the morning, the whole world beyond the windows of the Waffle House had been reduced to black glass, yellow light, and the long wet hiss of tires on asphalt.

Inside, the coffee was old but hot.

Marlene Vale stood behind the counter with one hip against the ice machine and watched a dead man stir cream into a cup he had asked for black.

He wore a John Deere cap and a denim jacket darkened at the shoulder where rain had soaked through. There was mud on his boots. Red clay. Not from the parking lot. Not from anywhere near Exit 143.

His hands shook around the spoon.

"You headed home?" Marlene asked.

The man looked up. His eyes were the washed-out blue of old winter sky.

"Reckon so," he said.

"Where's home?"

He blinked at her. The spoon ticked softly against the side of the mug.

"My wife'll be waiting."

That was not an answer, but Marlene had worked the graveyard shift for twenty-three years, and she knew the difference between a man dodging a question and a man who had misplaced the shape of his life.

She nodded like he had told her something useful.

"Then you finish that before it gets cold."

"Yes, ma'am."

He drank obediently.

On the grill, Leon scraped grease into the trap with slow, practiced strokes. Scrape, scrape, pause. Scrape, scrape, pause. He had the rhythm in his bones. They all did, if they lasted past their third winter. New hires came in thinking Waffle House was just work: orders shouted over steam, drunks after last call, truckers with red eyes, cops wanting their hash browns covered and smothered, church folk on Sunday, families fleeing hurricanes with pets in laundry baskets and children asleep under tables.

They learned.

Some quit before they knew what they had learned.

Some stayed long enough that the knowing settled under language, where instinct lived.

"You see that?" Leon asked without looking up.

Marlene followed his eyes to the front windows.

For half a second, there was no reflection in the glass.

Not the counter. Not the booths. Not the row of little globe lights over the register. Only dark outside and something pale standing in it, too tall for a man and too thin for weather.

Then headlights swept across the parking lot, and the windows remembered themselves.

"I see rain," Marlene said.

Leon snorted.

"Rain got elbows now?"

"Maybe upstate rain does."

"Ain't upstate."

No, it was not.

Upstate rain came down cold and mean over ridges and cutbanks. This rain had come from somewhere under the ground. Marlene could smell it when the door opened: wet stone, black leaves, old metal, a sweetness like meat kept too long in a unplugged freezer.

The dead man in the John Deere cap kept stirring his coffee.

"You want me to call somebody?" Leon asked.

"No."

"You sure?"

"Ain't nobody to call for rain."

Leon scraped the grill again. Scrape, scrape, pause.

The bell above the door rang.

The girl who came in could not have been more than seventeen. Maybe eighteen if life had done its usual cruel work and made her face younger by making her eyes older. She had a gray hoodie pulled up over hair plastered dark against her cheeks. Her jeans were torn at one knee. There was blood on her left shoe, not much, but enough for Marlene to see the way it refused to dilute in the water dripping onto the tile.

The girl stood just inside the door and shivered.

Nobody spoke.

That was the first warning.

There were four living customers in the restaurant besides the girl: a state trooper in the back booth pretending not to sleep, two roofing boys splitting a waffle by the register, and Miss Elspeth Grady, who had been alive when Marlene clocked in and dead for most of an hour since, though she had not yet accepted it. Every one of them looked at the girl and then looked away.

Even the dead man stopped stirring.

Marlene came around the counter.

"Evening, honey."

The girl swallowed. Her lips were pale.

"I need a phone."

"Phone's right here." Marlene pointed to the wall by the restrooms, though there had not been a working pay phone there since 2009. "But first you come sit down."

"I can't."

"You can."

"No, I mean—"

The girl looked back through the glass door.

Rain hammered the parking lot. Beyond the yellow spill of the sign, the world was black and moving.

Marlene understood three things at once.

First: the girl was alive.

Second: the blood on her shoe was not hers.

Third: something had followed her right up to the threshold and stopped there, confused by the light.

That last part was interesting.

Most things that hunted at night understood doors. Doors were old. Doors had rules older than locks, older than hinges, older than the first plank laid against the mouth of a cave. A door said in and out. A door said ours and not ours. A door said knock, ask, enter if permitted, or be refused.

But some hungers were too young or too stupid or too full of themselves to know that.

Those were the dangerous ones.

"Leon," Marlene said.

"Already on it."

He cracked two eggs onto the grill.

The girl flinched at the sound.

"What's your name?" Marlene asked.

"Kayla."

A ripple passed through the room.

Not visible. Not sound exactly. More like the way a house settles after midnight, except every cup on the counter trembled once, and the old coffee in the pots darkened by a shade.

Names were handles.

Marlene kept her face calm.

"Kayla what?"

The girl hesitated.

Good. Some instinct still alive in her.

"Just Kayla."

"Just Kayla, you come sit at the counter. Third stool from the register."

"Why?"

"Because that's where I'm telling you to sit."

The roofing boys looked at each other. One of them had a tattoo of a skull smoking a cigarette on his forearm and the open, eager stupidity of a man who liked to see a scene unfold as long as it did not involve him paying for damage.

"Damn," he muttered. "She in trouble?"

Marlene turned her head.

"Eat your waffle, sweetheart."

He ate his waffle.

Kayla crossed the tile in short, careful steps, as if afraid the floor might object to her weight. When she passed the dead man in the John Deere cap, he looked at her with sudden terrible pity.

"Don't let him in," he said.

Kayla froze.

"Who?"

The dead man opened his mouth. Closed it. His spoon rolled from the saucer and clattered to the counter.

Marlene picked it up.

"You drink your coffee, Mr. Pell."

He frowned.

"Pell?"

"That's right."

"Is that my name?"

"For tonight."

He considered that, then nodded. The dead could be easier than the living if you gave them something to hold.

Kayla sat on the third stool from the register.

Leon slid a plate in front of her without being asked: scrambled eggs, toast, hash browns scattered and covered, bacon arranged in two neat strips like a peace offering. He set down a fork. Then a napkin. Then a little paper cup of apple juice from the kid's menu.

Kayla stared at it.

"I don't have money."

"Didn't ask."

"I can't eat."

"You can."

"Something happened."

"I know."

The girl looked up sharply.

Marlene poured coffee into a clean mug and set it beside the plate. "Cream?"

"I don't drink coffee."

"Tonight you do."

Outside, something tapped on the glass.

Once.

The state trooper woke all at once, hand going to the gun on his belt. His eyes were wide, and for a moment Marlene saw him at eight years old, hiding under a kitchen table while his father broke every cabinet door in the house. Fear did that sometimes. Peeled people down to the first shape terror had made of them.

"Don't," Marlene said.

The trooper's hand stopped.

"Ma'am?"

"You draw that in here, it'll be paperwork all night."

He blinked. Shame came into his face, then irritation, then obedience. He took his hand off the gun.

"Yes, ma'am."

Leon did not turn around.

Scrape, scrape, pause.

The thing outside tapped again.

This time, one of the roofing boys laughed under his breath.

"What the hell is that?"

His friend leaned to see.

Marlene caught a flash of white beyond the window. A suggestion of a face where no face had any reason to be. Too many teeth, or maybe only the idea of teeth. A shape practicing personhood from bad memory.

The roofing boy stood.

"Sit down," Marlene said.

"I just wanna—"

"Sit. Down."

There was no thunder. No dramatic swell of music. The lights did not flicker.

But the room changed.

The air gained weight. The yellow sign outside buzzed louder. The coffee pots ticked on their warmers like small clocks counting toward a verdict. Leon's spatula paused above the grill.

The boy sat.

He looked surprised to find himself doing it.

Marlene took a breath through her nose.

Ozone. Wet stone. Spoiled sweetness.

And under it, faint but steady, the smell of batter, coffee, bacon grease, bleach water, hot metal, and the particular tired hope of people eating under fluorescent light after midnight.

That smell had power.

Not the kind preachers shouted about. Not the kind men in black suits locked in federal basements tried to measure and name and weaponize. Older than that. Simpler.

Come in.

Sit down.

Eat.

Warm yourself.

The dark is outside for now.

That ritual had worn many skins. Cave fire. Longhouse hearth. Inn table. Public house. Kitchen during a storm. Roadside diner glowing yellow beside the interstate while rain tried to erase the world.

Marlene did not know all of that in words.

She knew the girl needed to eat.

She knew the thing outside did not get to come in just because it was hungry.

She knew Leon needed to keep the grill going.

She knew the dead man needed cream.

That was enough.

Kayla picked up the fork with shaking fingers.

"Good," Marlene said. "Small bites."

"I hit him," Kayla whispered.

"Who?"

"My stepdad."

There it was.

The rain seemed to lean closer.

"He was doing something in the basement," Kayla said. "He had candles. Not like church candles. Red ones. And there was a bowl. And pictures of me. Pictures from when I was little. I wasn't supposed to be home, but Jamie's mom got sick and I came back early. He was saying my name. My whole name. Over and over."

The coffee in Kayla's mug rippled.

Marlene laid one hand over it.

"Don't say the rest."

Kayla nodded quickly.

"He saw me. He smiled like he was happy I was there. Like that made it easier. He said it was already paid for. He said I didn't have to be scared because I wouldn't be around for most of it."

The state trooper muttered something that was either a curse or a prayer.

Marlene ignored him.

"What did you hit him with?"

"A hammer."

"Good girl."

Kayla made a small broken sound.

"I think I killed him."

"Maybe."

"You don't care?"

"I care plenty. Eat."

Outside, the thing scratched down the glass, long and slow.

The dead man in the John Deere cap began to cry.

"Don't let him in," he said again.

"Nobody's letting anybody in," Marlene said.

Miss Elspeth Grady lifted her head from the booth where she had been sitting with an untouched slice of pecan pie cooling in front of her.

"Marlene?"

"Yes, Miss Elspeth."

"Am I dead?"

Marlene looked at her.

The old woman sat very straight, church purse clutched in both hands, lipstick perfect despite the heart attack that had taken her quietly between one bite and the next. Marlene had been giving her time. There was no kindness in rushing the dead unless something worse was coming.

Something worse had come.

"Yes, ma'am," Marlene said. "I'm sorry."

Miss Elspeth sighed.

"Well. I thought so. My Arthur came by a minute ago, but he wouldn't sit."

"Arthur always was restless."

"He looked younger."

"They do sometimes."

Miss Elspeth looked toward the window. The thing outside pressed something like a palm against the glass.

"That is not Arthur."

"No, ma'am."

"Want me to do something about it?"

Kayla stared at the old woman.

Marlene considered.

The dead had weight if they remembered themselves right. Miss Elspeth had taught second grade for forty-one years, sung alto in the church choir for fifty-three, buried a husband, two brothers, and one son, and once slapped a county commissioner so hard at a school board meeting that the man forgot which lie he had been telling.

There were worse allies.

"Not yet," Marlene said.

Miss Elspeth nodded and opened her purse.

The roofing boy with the skull tattoo whispered, "What the actual fuck."

His friend shushed him.

Good.

Learning was happening.

The door rattled in its frame.

Once.

Twice.

Not a knock. Not yet. Testing.

Leon rang the order bell.

Once.

Marlene turned.

He had set a plate in the service window. One waffle. Golden, perfect, steaming. Butter melting in the little square pockets. Syrup on the side.

"For who?" Marlene asked.

Leon's face was calm, but his knuckles were white around the edge of the counter.

"Don't know."

Marlene looked at the plate.

The waffle smelled wrong.

Not rotten. Worse. Empty. Like hunger wearing sweetness.

The girl slid off the stool.

"I need to go."

Marlene put a hand on her shoulder and pushed her gently back down.

"No, you don't."

"He's here."

"Your stepdad?"

Kayla shook her head.

"No. What he called."

At the door, something spoke in a voice made of rainwater running through a grave.

"Kayla Lynn Whitcomb."

The full name hit the room like a brick through glass.

Every light flickered.

The coffee pots boiled over. The dead man shouted and covered his ears. The trooper drew his gun after all, but the gun slid from his hand, skittered across the tile, and came to rest under the counter where Marlene's sensible black shoe pinned it gently to the floor.

Nobody bled on the threshold.

That was the second rule.

Names were handles.

Full names were chains.

Kayla folded around herself, both arms locked across her stomach.

"Don't let it take me," she said.

Marlene felt something in the building wake.

Not all at once. The Waffle House was not a beast. It did not have eyes to open or wings to spread. It woke like a place remembers why it was made.

The grill hissed.

The sign buzzed.

The ice machine clunked.

The old jukebox in the corner, which had been unplugged since a drunk Clemson fan spilled sweet tea into it six years back, lit up blue and gold.

A song began to play.

No guitar at first. Just a low hum, a woman's voice far away, and the sound of rain on a tin roof.

Miss Elspeth smiled.

"Oh," she said. "I know this one."

Marlene did not.

But her mouth did.

"Leon," she said. "Salt."

He tossed the shaker without looking. She caught it.

The thing at the door said Kayla's full name again.

This time the glass bowed inward.

The state trooper scrambled back from his booth. The roofing boys ducked under the table. Mr. Pell, dead and damp and shaking, stood up from his stool.

"I remember," he said.

Marlene kept her eyes on the door.

"Do you?"

"Truck jackknifed. Mile marker 139. I went through the windshield."

"I'm sorry."

"I was bringing my daughter a crib."

"I know."

He looked at Kayla.

Something settled in his face. Not peace. Usefulness.

"My daughter was named Kayla."

"A lot of girls are."

"Still."

He stepped between the girl and the door.

Miss Elspeth rose from her booth, purse in hand.

"Children," she said, with the particular disappointment of a teacher finding gum under a desk.

The roofing boys looked up.

"Not you," she snapped.

They ducked again.

The thing outside knocked.

Once.

A proper knock this time.

That was worse.

Marlene walked to the door.

The pale shape bent down until its almost-face was level with hers through the glass. It had learned eyes from something dead. It had learned a mouth from something afraid. It wore Kayla's stepfather's smile because that was the last human mask available to it, but it did not understand how smiles worked, and the expression stretched too far.

Behind Marlene, Leon kept the rhythm.

Scrape, scrape, pause.

The jukebox hummed.

Coffee is mercy.

Salt is law.

Marlene did not know if the words came from the song or from under her own tongue.

The thing raised one long finger and tapped the glass.

"Invited," it said.

"Not by this house."

"Paid for."

"Not to this house."

"Mine."

"No."

Its mouth widened.

The glass fogged around the word it breathed next.

"Hungry."

Marlene almost laughed.

Not because it was funny. Because it was always hunger with things like this. Hunger dressed up as covenant, hunger wearing scripture, hunger with paperwork, hunger with a family name in its mouth. Men called it destiny when they sold their children. Things called it law when they came to collect.

Marlene had served hunger every night of her working life.

Hunger was not special.

"Then you can order like everybody else," she said.

The room went still.

Leon stopped scraping.

Miss Elspeth closed her purse with a click.

Kayla made a sound too small to be a word.

The thing outside tilted its head.

Marlene unlocked the door.

"Marlene," Leon said.

"I know."

She opened the door exactly six inches. Rain blew cold across her shoes.

The thing did not cross the threshold. It could not, not yet. Not while the door was only partly open, not while Marlene stood there in her black work pants and yellow name tag with one hand on the frame.

"You want in," she said, "you state your order."

Its borrowed eyes twitched.

"Kayla Lynn Whitcomb."

"Not on the menu."

"Mine."

"Already told you no."

"Paid."

"Then take it up with management."

The thing looked past her into the restaurant. Its attention moved over the trooper, the roofing boys, the dead, Leon, and finally Kayla. Wherever it looked, people seemed to dim a little, like candles in bad air.

Then it looked at the waffle in the service window.

Its mouth worked.

"Blue plate," it said.

Marlene's heart sank.

Behind the counter, Leon whispered, "Shit."

The third rule under the counter, written in red nail polish older than most of the staff, read:

Everybody gets coffee once.

Nobody bleeds on the threshold.

If they ask for the blue plate special, wake Marlene.

Nobody had written what Marlene was supposed to do if she was already awake.

For the first time that night, she was afraid.

Fear was fine. Fear kept fools alive when pride couldn't.

She closed the door.

The thing let her.

That frightened her more.

Marlene walked back to the counter, picked up the waffle, and carried it to the register. The plate was cold in her hands, though steam rose from it.

"Kayla," she said. "Listen to me."

The girl lifted her head.

"When I tell you to run, you go through the kitchen, out the back, straight to the dumpster. There is a drainage ditch behind it. You'll think you can't fit. You can. Follow the ditch until you see a dogwood tree split by lightning. Sit under it until morning. Do not answer anybody who calls your name. Not me. Not your mama. Not Jesus Christ Almighty. Understand?"

Kayla nodded, crying silently.

"Trooper."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Your cruiser got a shotgun?"

"Yes, ma'am."

"Won't help. Stay seated."

He sat.

"Boys."

Two pale faces appeared over the booth edge.

"When this is done, you will pay your check, tip properly, and never tell this story in a way that makes you sound brave."

Both boys nodded.

"Miss Elspeth."

"Yes, Marlene."

"If it gets past me, you teach it manners."

The old dead woman smiled.

"Gladly."

"Mr. Pell."

The dead trucker straightened.

"Ma'am."

"You still want to bring that crib home?"

His face broke.

"Yes."

"Then help me keep somebody's daughter alive."

He nodded.

Marlene took the salt shaker and turned it over above the waffle.

She poured until the golden squares were white.

Then she added coffee.

The hot liquid soaked into the salt, into the butter, into the bread. The smell turned sharp and dark, sweet and bitter, like a kitchen after a funeral.

The jukebox grew louder.

Coffee is mercy.

Salt is law.

Keep your shadow past the door.

Marlene carried the plate to the entrance.

The thing waited outside in the rain.

She opened the door all the way.

It smiled.

Marlene stepped aside.

"Come in."

The thing crossed the threshold.

Every window shattered inward.

Or tried to.

Glass burst from the frames and stopped in the air, each fragment hanging bright and sharp under the fluorescent lights. Rain froze with it, thousands of drops suspended like beads on invisible thread.

The roofing boys screamed.

Leon rang the bell.

Once.

Twice.

Three times.

Judgment entered the room without fanfare.

It did not look like God. It did not look like anything. For one unbearable second, the Waffle House contained a cave mouth, a longhouse fire, a public house lamp, a farmhouse kitchen, an inn yard full of muddy horses, a hospital cafeteria, a bus station vending machine, and every place anyone had ever sat down shaking while someone gave them something hot and said, there now, breathe.

The thing in the doorway recoiled.

Marlene saw it clearly then.

Not a demon, exactly. Not old enough. A hungry little prince from somewhere close beneath the skin of things, fattened on bargains made by men who wanted power over daughters, wives, congregations, employees, patients. It had been fed names for generations. It thought that made it royalty.

In Marlene's house, it was a customer.

And customers had rules.

She set the plate on the table nearest the door.

"Blue plate special," she said. "Eat."

The thing's head jerked toward her.

"Mine."

"You ordered."

"Mine."

"You ordered," Marlene repeated. "You came in. You sit. You eat what you're served."

It lunged for Kayla.

Or tried.

Leon was there.

He moved like a man reaching for a falling cup, no hurry, no flourish. One hand caught the thing by what might have been a wrist. The other pressed flat against its chest.

"No running in the restaurant," he said.

The thing hit the floor hard enough to crack tile.

Leon looked down at it, startled.

"Huh."

Miss Elspeth stepped forward and struck it across the face with her purse.

The sound was enormous.

"We do not grab at girls," she said.

Mr. Pell took Kayla by the shoulders, careful as a father carrying glass, and moved her behind the counter.

"Go," Marlene said.

Kayla ran.

The thing shrieked.

The sound made the dead remember dying and the living remember every room where they had ever been afraid. The trooper vomited into his hat. One roofing boy started praying. The other began to cry for his mother.

Marlene picked up the coffee pot.

It burned her palm.

She poured coffee over the thing's head.

"Everybody gets coffee once."

The scream stopped.

The thing looked up at her from the cracked tile. Coffee ran down its almost-face. Salt clung to its mouth.

For a moment, she saw Kayla's stepfather in it: a small, cruel man in a basement, mistaking access for ownership. Behind him, his father. Behind him, another man. Behind him, a line of men and women too, all of them offering up someone smaller because something had told them hunger was holiness if you dressed it right.

Marlene felt tired down to the marrow.

"Pay your tab," she said.

"No."

"Pay it."

"No."

The house leaned in.

Not physically. The walls stayed walls. The counter stayed Formica. The sign kept buzzing in the rain.

But every booth, every plate, every cup, every shift worked half-dead with exhaustion, every meal served during evacuation, every drunk kept from choking, every stranger allowed to sit until they stopped shaking, every waitress who had stood between trouble and the door because somebody had to, all of it leaned toward the thing on the floor.

It reached into its mouth and pulled out a name.

Not Kayla's.

An older one.

It looked like a strip of wet paper and a baby tooth and a curl of hair. It looked like a receipt.

Marlene took it with two fingers.

The thing shuddered.

Outside, somewhere behind the building, a girl splashed through the drainage ditch toward a dogwood split by lightning.

"You are no longer welcome in this house," Marlene said.

The thing crawled backward.

For the first time, it looked afraid.

Good.

"Door's there," she said.

It went.

Not vanished. Not destroyed. Things like that were rarely destroyed. It dragged itself through the door and into the rain, smaller than it had been when it entered, leaving no blood behind because the threshold had rules and Marlene was too tired to mop more than she had to.

The glass fell.

Not inward. Out.

Every shattered piece flew back into place with a sound like a thousand tiny bells. Rain resumed. The jukebox clicked off. The lights steadied.

For a while, nobody moved.

Then Leon said, "Order up," because there were eggs dying in the window and some habits were stronger than awe.

Marlene laughed once.

It hurt.

She looked at her hand. The palm was red where the coffee pot had burned her. In the center of the burn, a small white mark had formed. Not a cross. Not a symbol she knew. Something older than letters, maybe. Something like a door seen from far away.

She closed her fist.

The state trooper wiped his mouth with a napkin.

"Ma'am," he said, voice rough. "What do I put in my report?"

"Gas leak."

"There wasn't—"

She looked at him.

"Gas leak."

"Yes, ma'am."

The roofing boys crawled out from under the booth.

"We didn't see shit," said the one with the skull tattoo.

Marlene pointed at him.

"Language."

"Sorry."

"And you still owe me for that waffle."

"Yes, ma'am."

Miss Elspeth returned to her booth and picked up her fork.

"I suppose I won't finish this pie," she said.

Marlene's throat tightened.

"No, ma'am. I don't think you will."

"Shame. It was better than usual."

"I'll tell Leon."

"Don't. He'll get vain."

Leon, who had absolutely heard, smiled at the grill.

Mr. Pell stood near the counter, looking toward the windows. The rain had eased enough for the parking lot lights to show the wet black ribbon of interstate beyond the frontage road.

"Will she make it?" he asked.

Marlene looked past him, through the glass, past the yellow reflection of the sign.

Far off behind the restaurant, under the ditchline and the trees, something green and old had closed around a running girl like hands cupped around a match.

"Till morning," Marlene said.

"And after?"

"After is always harder."

Mr. Pell nodded.

The first gray suggestion of dawn touched the edge of the clouds.

He looked younger in that light. Or maybe only less wet.

"My daughter," he said. "She'll be waiting?"

Marlene wanted to lie.

She did not.

"I don't know."

He smiled a little.

"That's all right."

Miss Elspeth stood, purse over one arm.

"Come on, Mr. Pell. If there is somewhere to go, no sense making the lady hold the door all day."

The dead trucker looked at Marlene.

"Thank you for the coffee."

"You paid?"

He patted his pockets, embarrassed.

Marlene waved him off.

"On the house."

"Seems wrong."

"It ain't."

He considered that, then touched the brim of his cap.

Miss Elspeth took his elbow.

Together, they walked toward the door. It opened without the bell ringing. Morning, thin and colorless, waited outside.

They stepped through.

The door closed.

The bell rang once, very softly, after they were gone.

Leon turned the bacon.

The trooper went out to sit in his cruiser and not write a report for twenty minutes. The roofing boys paid cash, tipped forty dollars on eighteen, and left without looking at the windows. Kayla did not come back, which meant she had listened.

At 6:12, the first breakfast customer came in complaining about the rain.

He was a salesman from Ohio with wet cuffs and no idea that the tile under his shoes had cracked around the outline of something that should never have learned to knock.

He sat at the counter and frowned at the menu.

"Coffee," he said. "Black. And eggs over medium."

Marlene looked at him for a long second.

Her hand ached.

Her feet hurt.

She had lost something in the night. She could feel the absence of it, though she did not yet know what shape it had been. A memory, maybe. A small one. Someone's laugh. A song her mother used to hum. The color of a dress she had worn once to a dance. The house always took little things first.

Everything had a cost.

Even Waffle House at 3 AM.

She poured the coffee anyway.

"Cream?" she asked.

"Said black."

"I heard you."

She set the mug in front of him.

Outside, morning gathered itself over the interstate. Inside, the grill hissed, the coffee steamed, and the yellow sign kept burning over the door like a promise nobody had made and everybody needed.
